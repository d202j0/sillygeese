For the Enterprise in Permanent Motion

Digital Dynamism

Digital transformation has no endpoint. There is no level five, no completion date, no moment of arrival. Organisations keep chasing that moment.

It never comes.

Being dynamic is a disposition. The capacity to keep moving well.

Why now

AI is the proof, not the destination

AI has arrived. Many organisations are figuring out what to do with it, while their customers, vendors and colleagues are all battling with what it means for them as well.

I believe the organisations that will navigate AI successfully are not the ones with the best models or the biggest budgets. They are the ones who are dynamic.

The operating models, methodologies and practices inherited from prior generations, and even from just last year, will not hold. We already struggle with waterfall governance applied to hybrid-agile efforts, and transformation programmes with completion dates that can never complete. Add consensus-based processes and we're too slow for windows of opportunity that change every few months.

AI exacerbates all of those realities, but AI itself is not the problem to solve. It is just the current and most urgent need. It is today's context. Being dynamic and what I believe that means predates AI and will outlast whatever comes next. The principles hold regardless of era.

The outcome

Freedom

Freedom itself, in this context, is not something you can just practise. It is the outcome you get from applying the four principles outlined below. A dynamic organisation is free - free to make any decision, at any moment, unconstrained from what came before. No sunk cost should be too heavy to abandon. No vendor relationship too entrenched to leave. No belief too established.

Being dynamic gives you freedom from the past.

The practice

Four principles

Everything I have suggested below is dependent on the others. It's not a sequence or specific flow, it's a system I have tried to articulate based on my own observations and experiences.

Without honest, shared visibility into where an organisation actually is - not where the last status report suggested, not where the roadmap would like it would be - every decision runs on gut or on a borrowed instinct. Neither serves the organisation and leads to inconsistency. Context is the only reliable foundation for directionally correct decisions, and it has to flow to everyone, not just the leadership layer.

Most organisations mistake strategy for context. A company strategy pitched at such high altitude that almost any initiative can claim alignment with it, only provides cover, not context. Functions develop their own versions - marketing, sales, supply, R&D, technology - each coherent internally, disconnected from the others. No single thread runs through the organisation.

Context is a living, shared understanding of where the organisation actually is - maintained continuously, owned collectively, and visible to everyone who needs to act on it.

Towards

The clarity to make confident decisions. Always knowing where you actually are.

Away from

Never again running blind, on gut, or on an instinct that does not fit your reality.

In practice

When it breaks down

The functional silo

A global organisation runs a company strategy pitched so high that almost any initiative can claim alignment with it. Each function builds its own coherent version, disconnected from the rest. It surfaces as a recurring S&OP argument between sales and supply — sales claiming ownership of the total forecast, supply claiming the baseline. Neither wrong by their own logic. But there is no shared definition of reality that predates the meeting, so nothing resolves. Every decision downstream is built on a contested foundation.

When it breaks down

The watermelon project

A large technology programme runs for years. Status reports consistently green — budget on track, schedule on track, requirements met. But the people who have to use it are not happy. The technology team operates on its context of delivery milestones and specifications; the business on its context of commercial outcomes and user experience. The two never genuinely meet. Green on the outside, red on the inside. A thirty-million, three-year investment was eventually replaced by a fifteen-month, one-and-a-half-million rebuild — once the contexts were joined and the courage was found to change direction. The team built from that shared understanding is still operating and evolving six years later.

When it works

Door number three

Where context works, a technology leader and a business leader genuinely partner — not to manage a relationship, but to build and maintain a single view of reality neither could hold alone. The difference is rarely data or technology. It is a shared understanding of priorities: what matters most, what comes first, what the organisation is collectively willing to put last. Door number three — the option that serves the whole enterprise rather than any single function — gets considered, because the people who can see it are finally in the room.

Speed without commitment produces fast decisions that die in implementation. Commitment without speed produces decisions that arrive too late to have an impact.

Most organisations will treat speed and commitment as a trade-off. The things that build commitment - consultation, debate, bringing people along - are naturally slow. So an organisation will move too fast and leave the wrong people behind, or build consensus so slowly it misses the window of opportunity.

A dynamic organisation can produce commitment at speed, with the alignment done up front through shared context. Everyone is part of the same reality. With that pre-built trust, commitment does not start from scratch in a meeting room. With clear decision ownership, the right people present (and the wrong ones not...), and psychological safety, people will commit faster.

Towards

Decisions that move fast and stick. Commitment that becomes momentum.

Away from

Never watching a good decision die because the wrong people were in the room, or the right people were not ready.

In practice

When it breaks down

The leader who never said yes

Some leaders frame indecision as diligence. Every proposal is met with questions rather than answers; every request for a decision produces a request for more information. The people around them adapt — learning to frame conversations not as "can we do this" but as "we are going to do this unless you tell us not to." The workaround works, but at a cost: extra cycles, unnecessary stress, and a slow erosion of confidence in the more junior people caught in the middle. They were not missing anything. Indecision was the issue. But indecision rarely announces itself as such.

When it breaks down

The RFP that answered a dead question

In the early days of automated marketing and customer data platforms, a global organisation decided to run a full procurement process. A small team travelled the world for months collecting requirements. An RFP ran for months more. The team flew around the world again, ensuring every stakeholder felt heard. The obvious choice — visible to most informed observers from day one — was eventually selected. Contract negotiation ran for months. By the time the platform went live, the original goals had dissolved and the organisational energy was spent. And while the committee was on its journey, the people closest to the need had not waited: local teams with budget autonomy had engaged agencies to fill the gap. The organisation paid twice. The process was designed to prevent a bad decision. It prevented any decision.

When it works

Context shared aggressively, decision made cleanly

A clear-eyed commercial leader identified customer experience as the right lever for growth — specifically order availability and shipping transparency for a seasonal product. The context was simple and shared without ambiguity: customers who could not see what was happening with their orders were hedging by ordering from competitors. Returns were high, trust was low. With that context established and a leader willing to commit, a small team designed and built a custom forecasting and allocation process in months. Customers gained full transparency. Order quality improved. Returns fell. The window existed, the context was clear, the decision was made — and the outcome was delivered.

Change takes an emotional toll that cannot be managed away with a communications plan. The person who spent three years becoming an expert in the platform you are deciding to abandon does not only have a skills gap to close with training. They have invested in their identity, taken a stake in their career path and this will be a legitimate loss. By being transparent you acknowledge that openly, give it room and provide real support. Suppressing it, or simply trying to train around it does not make it disappear - it can create resistance.

Transparency runs in two ways, and both are hard. Receiving decisions without understanding the context - budgets cut, projects delayed, people let go - leaves people unable to explain it to themselves or others. Being fully read into a situation and asked to deliver that decision to those who are not, and cannot be - you hold the context but are unable to share it - is its own particular difficulty. Both erode trust when handled poorly.

A dynamic organisation does not pretend these tensions do not exist. It gives people time to grieve when something they cared about changes, and it offers genuine acknowledgement that something of value has been lost - even when the decision is right - it is now part of the shared context. Everyone matters here, not just the leadership layers. The people closest to the work hold the most critical institutional knowledge and the most legitimate attachment to what is changing. Losing them emotionally is dangerous to progress.

Towards

A team that stays whole through change. People who bring their full capability because they feel genuinely valued.

Away from

The quiet attrition, the passive resistance, the institutional knowledge that walks out when people feel discarded.

People are not all motivated the same way. Some want to hear why something is better, the vision, the upside and the possibilities ahead. That's what makes them tick (I'm one of those). Others will need to hear what they are being saved from, the risk, the problem or the pain and frustration being removed. Leading in only one direction can leave half the team unmoved. A dynamic organisation can speak both languages with equal conviction and it is all available within the current context.

Most change programmes will pick one mode and that will drive the story. The vision deck is all towards - here is the bright future, the better place to be, the faster way to act and why it's exciting. Half the room will be thinking "but it works for me right now, it's not broken" and never have the motivation to commit. The dynamic organisation will be as effective at painting the bright future over the burning platform(s) at the same time, and is able to speak to either person in any moment.

Feeling like you're an integral part of the solution is itself motivating. When enough people have the context of why a decision was taken, and it speaks to their motivations, they stop being a recipient of change and become active participants. That shift - from managed to engaged - is what separates organisations that can sustain dynamism from those that will exhaust themselves, and everyone in it.

Towards

A workforce genuinely energised by the direction of travel — each person hearing the version of the truth that lands for them.

Away from

Half the organisation never convinced, because the message only ever spoke to the other half.

Boundaries

What this is not

  • Not a maturity model. There is no level five.
  • Not a transformation methodology. There is no completion.
  • Not a technology framework. The technology is incidental.
  • Not a consulting product. No methodology to sell, no certification to award.
  • Not a personal brand. The work speaks.

You do not achieve Digital Dynamism. You practise it.

Close

Agile is something you are told to be. Dynamic is something you become.
